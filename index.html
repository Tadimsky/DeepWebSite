The Deep Web

An ethical, technical and social enquiry into Tor and the Deep Web.

Tor and the Deep Web

Tor (The Onion Router), is free software that allows users to browse the internet anonymously. Tor was originally a project developed by the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory in conjunction with DARPA. The military currently uses Tor for intelligence gathering, espionage operations, and communication. Tor is also used for purposes outside of the military such as by activists, by journalists in countries that restrict reporting certain topics, whistleblowers, and normal people who may not want to be tracked for a variety of reasons.

Tor has recently been the center of quite a bit of controversy about its usages throughout the world, notably its advantage in facilitating illegal activity such as drug and weapons trade, sale of illegal items, and assassination contracts.

How it Works

Tor works by transmitting data through multiple nodes inside the network. These nodes are every computer connected to the Tor network.
Each time a packet is sent, it randomly selects a path between these nodes so that no pattern can be observer by an outsider.
Each node knows only the address of the node that is sending it data and the address of the next node to send to. Each layer is encrypted and so this encryption is peeled away, like an onion.

If the desired destination in in the deep web, or is a Hidden Service, then the traffic never leaves the Tor network. However, if the final destination is a 'normal' website, then the traffic exits via an exit node.
Exit nodes are nodes that allow traffic to flow through them to the outside internet. This is the point at which servers see your IP address coming from and so is effectively your public IP address.
